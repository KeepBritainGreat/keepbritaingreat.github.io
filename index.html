We make Europe's laws


At the moment, we help control the laws of 27 other European countries.[1]

By leaving, we're giving this power up.

Under the proposed deal, we'll also have to start letting those countries write our laws for us.

We were told we didn't have soverignity. We've actually been tricked into giving it up.

[1] Full Fact: EU laws are decided by the European Parliament, which British citizens elect ministers to, and the Council, which the British government is a member of.

Freedom to go anywhere


Thousands of British retirees use their European freedom of movement to spend their long winters in places like Spain, France, and Italy to enjoy the warmer climate.[2]

Brexit-loving politicians want to take this right away from Brits who've worked hard all their lives to enjoy it.

If you retire after Britain leaves the EU, you'll never be able to have the experience of spending your winters in a warmer country - at least not without many complicated restrictions.

[2] Office of National Statistics: 1.6m British citizens spent 1-12 months abroad in the year to June 2016. Over-55s made up around 30% of all people who emigrated for this length of time in the same period. This suggests around 500,000 British over-55s spent between 1-12 months abroad in the year to June 2016. For this period Spain, France and Italy were in the top 10 most popular destinations for all short-term migrants.

We profit from EU immigration


EU immigrants contribute a massive 34% more in taxes than they take out. They're also highly likely to work in public services such as the NHS.[3][4]

Non-EU immigrants only contribute 2% more than they take out on average.[3]

Hard Brexiters want to replace EU immigration with non-EU immigration, and make our country poorer in the process.

Britain has remained a global economic powerhouse by allowing EU citizens to live and work alongside us.

[3] Centre for Research & Analysis of Migration, UCL: Study conducted November 2013. Figures refer to EEA and non-EEA immigrants who arrived in the period 2000-2011. BBC News summary.
[4] Office of National Statistics: About 362,000 EU immigrants work in the "Public admin, education and health" sector. This is the third most popular sector for EU immigrants to work in, and the most popular for those from the western EU14 countries.

Value for money


Britain pays £9 billion into the EU budget every year.[5]

It sounds like a lot, but it's only about 6% of the annual cost of the NHS, 10% of state pensions, and about the same as we spend on foreign aid - money we give away as charity.[6]

In return, we get a leading seat at the table of the world's biggest trading bloc. The economic boost we get from this is worth far more than its cost.

We also get EU investment in parts of the country usually ignored by Westminster, like the Midlands, the North of England and Wales.

For example, the new Midland Metropolitan Hospital near Birmingham recently received £108m in EU funding towards its construction.[7]

The cost of EU membership is a steal compared to the benefits we get from it.

[5] Full Fact: £9bn was the UK's net contribution to the EU budget in 2017.
[6] HM Treasury: In the fiscal year 2017/18, £145.8bn was spent on 'Health', £93.8bn on state pensions, and £8.6bn on overseas aid. [7] European Commission: The funding for the Midlands Metropolitan Hospital is just one example of EU funding for projects in British communities.

Lies and trickery


Some people think that immigration has caused the housing crisis and longer waits in their GP's surgery.

We now know that these things were caused by underfunding and austerity by past British governments, as well as a range of other complex factors.[8][9]

Brexiters blamed our problems on immigrants to trick honest British people into voting to make their own country poorer.

[8] The Guardian: This article notes an LSE report which found that two thirds of housing demand is created not by immigration but by a lack of social housing being built, among other factors.
[9] Care Quality Commission: The latest State of Care report concludes that rising demand is due to our aging population, an increasing number of diseases and problems retaining staff, while a lack of funding has made solving these problems more challenging.
